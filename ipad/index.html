The Problem

We built the problem.
Now let's fix it.

Every tech brand in the world runs the same campaign. Beautiful people. Perfect light. Nobody is hunched. Nobody has a stiff neck. Nobody looks like they've been scrolling for four hours.

But that's exactly what's happening.

Tech neck is not a fringe concern. Almost every smartphone user has felt it. And every brand in the category is pretending it doesn't exist.

The Insight

The phone pulls you down.
The iPad lets you stay up.

A phone is designed to be held. Which means it's designed to be looked down at. The screen size, the weight, the way you grip it, everything about it puts your head at an angle.

An iPad on a stand changes the geometry entirely. Bigger screen. Natural viewing distance. Eye level if you want it.

The product already solves the problem. This campaign just names it.

The Strategic Argument

Why this is a reasonable campaign for Apple to run.

Apple's strongest brand moves have always named the thing everyone else was ignoring. "1,000 songs in your pocket" named the awkwardness of carrying CDs. "The computer for the rest of us" named the intimidation of PCs.

The pattern is the same every time. Find what the whole industry is avoiding. Say it out loud. Let the product be the answer.

The brand that names tech neck first owns the conversation. Right now, nobody's claimed it.

The moment is already here. Health organisations noticed before anyone in tech did. The National Spine Health Foundation ran a "Look Up" campaign in 2023. Australia dedicated an entire Spinal Health Week to it in 2025. The conversation is happening without Apple in it.

  • People are already spending money on this. The posture-correction market hit $1.24B in 2024, heading to $2B by 2030.
  • UK searches for "tech neck" nearly doubled in a single month. Public awareness is accelerating fast.
  • Sitting 6+ hours a day raises neck pain odds by 88%. That's just a regular workday.
  • Women report higher rates of neck pain than men, and are a core iPad demographic.

Every inch forward doubles the load.

At 45 degrees, the average phone-scrolling angle, your neck is carrying 49 pounds. For hours. Every day.

iPad isn't just a bigger screen. It's the first screen that actually moves with you.

Straighten Up.

Brand Responsibility

Who this campaign isn't talking to. And why that matters.

This campaign is about a preventable habit. The strain caused by holding a small screen and looking down at it for hours. That's all it is.

It is not a claim about spine health. It is not for people living with spinal conditions, scoliosis, kyphosis, chronic pain, or anything that affects posture structurally. That distinction needs to live in the casting, the language, and the framing from the start. Not as a disclaimer at the end.

Not everyone can just straighten up. The campaign has to know that.

A good campaign knows exactly who it's speaking to. Being clear about that isn't a limitation. It's what keeps the work honest.
What the campaign says
  • Phone habits create unnecessary strain on a healthy spine
  • iPad on a stand changes the geometry of how you use a screen
  • Small changes in device use have a real physical impact
What the campaign doesn't say
  • iPad fixes or treats spinal conditions
  • Bad posture is a character flaw
  • "Just straighten up" is an option for everyone
