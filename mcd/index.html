A McDonald's Brand Strategy Pitch

I started noticing how people talk about their McDonald's runs, and it's never about the food. It's about the day they just survived. That thread turned into this.

The Question
What if McDonald's stopped chasing celebrations and started owning the ordinary days instead?
How I Got There
Talked to people, pulled data, looked at how different generations describe the same McDonald's run and why they all say the same thing.
The Idea
McDonald's as the reward for ordinary effort. Not the biggest reward. The one that's always there.
What I Made
Positioning, a film concept, outdoor ads, a social campaign, and tone guidelines, all from one insight.
The Context

We are in a productivity-obsessed era.

We see the success, but the effort behind it stays invisible.

Social media amplifies milestones, but most days are just about getting through it.

Fast food keeps competing on price, value, and menu innovation. Nobody owns endurance.

The Reality

McDonald's is not niche.

This isn't a youth trend. This is everyone, across ages, across life stages, all the time. And if the average person is going weekly, this isn't an occasional treat anymore, it's something else entirely.

It is emotional infrastructure.

The Category

QSR competes on everything except this.

  • Who has the best deal.
  • Who has the newest menu item.
  • Who has the funniest Twitter account.
  • Who can out-personality the other.

But nobody's talking about how people actually feel at the end of the day. That's the white space.

The Human Insight

Across qualitative research, a recurring theme appears.

Fast food gets framed as a treat. Parents describe "treating" their children, teens describe impulse visits after seeing an ad, and adults admit continued attachment despite being fully aware of the health conversation.

The words change depending on who you ask, but the function is always the same.

Permission. Reward. Relief.

Not because something extraordinary happened.

Because something ordinary ended.

The Tension

Modern culture celebrates the big win.

But most people are just trying to get through the day.

McDonald's doesn't need to own the finish line. It owns the pause.

The Idea

The Reward That's
Always There.

Not for your biggest achievement, but for the day you just made it through.

Creative Direction

Restrained. Observational. Underplayed.

The work avoids sentimental music swells, heroic arcs, and generational clichés, and comes back to real moments observed without commentary.

The product shows up casually, the copy stays quiet, and people feel seen rather than sold to. Whether it's film, a billboard, or a social post, the rule is the same: say less.

Why This Works

The numbers back up the feeling.

Strategically

  • 87% of US households already go to McDonald's, this isn't niche, it's universal.
  • People visit 54 times a year on average. That's weekly. That's habit, not splurge.
  • Every age group describes the same thing differently but the emotional function is identical.
  • Revenue is steady year-round. This isn't seasonal. It's constant.

Creatively

  • "Treat" stops meaning childish indulgence and starts meaning emotional reset.
  • McDonald's gets to be important without being loud about it.
  • This isn't a campaign, it's a platform that can run for years.
The Logic Chain

How I got from a hunch to an idea.

Everything in this pitch started with one observation. Here's the path from there:

01
The Human Need
People don't eat McDonald's because they're hungry. They eat it because the day took something out of them and they need a small act of permission.
02
What People Believe
Fast food is framed as a "treat," something indulgent, childish, and occasional that you earn through achievement or deserve as a splurge.
03
The Cultural Tension
We live in a productivity-obsessed era that celebrates milestones but ignores maintenance. Social media amplifies wins, and nobody really acknowledges the effort of just getting through an ordinary day.
04
The Strategic Opportunity
McDonald's has 87% household penetration and 54 weekly visits per year, which means this isn't occasional indulgence. It's habitual emotional behavior, and no competitor in QSR owns that kind of endurance.
05
The Idea
Reposition McDonald's as the reward for ordinary effort, not the biggest reward, but the one that's always there. The emotional constant in a culture that only celebrates the exceptional.

Thesis: McDonald's shouldn't fight for the celebratory moment. It should own the recovery. Being everywhere isn't a weakness, it's the whole point. When something is that present in people's lives, it can be the thing that's always there for you.

What I'd recommend: Build something long-term, not a campaign but a platform. Reframe "treat" from guilty pleasure to quiet reset. Show the product without glamorizing it. Keep the copy simple. Make people feel seen, not sold to.

What This Isn't

Things I deliberately left out.

Not This

  • Chasing Gen Z with memes.
  • Playing the nostalgia card.
  • Making it about holidays or big moments.
  • Celebrating milestones.

Because

McDonald's doesn't need to chase a moment. It already lives in the everyday. The whole point of this strategy is to protect that truth, not inflate it.

The behavior was already there. I just reframed it.

The Risk

McDonald's has always leaned into nostalgia and celebration.

This pitch goes somewhere quieter, and that takes nerve.

The Hard Part

The instinct will always be to add warmth, sentimentality, a swelling soundtrack. Every stakeholder will want to make it bigger. The discipline is in keeping it small.

Why It's Worth It

In a category where everyone shouts, the brand that speaks quietly earns trust. Restraint isn't low energy, it's confidence.

Final Positioning

McDonald's doesn't reward greatness. It just shows up when the day is over, across ages, across life stages, across ordinary days.

The Reward That's Always There.

Reflection

Why I made this.

Nobody asked me to make a McDonald's pitch. I just kept thinking about how everyone I know describes their McDonald's run the same way, like "I deserved that," regardless of age or context. That felt like something worth pulling apart.

What I Practiced
Listening to how people actually talk about a brand and then finding the pattern underneath.
The Hard Part
Taking "treat," a word everyone uses, and proving it means something deeper than indulgence.
What Excited Me
Figuring out the tone. How do you make a brand this big feel quiet and personal?
What I Learned
One real insight can power an entire system, film, outdoor, social, all of it, if you trust it enough.

The thinking is the work, and the restraint is what makes it land.
